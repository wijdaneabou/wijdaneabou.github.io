Generative AI in Computer Vision

For years, computer vision was about understanding images. Now, it creates them. This article explains the three architectures behind that shift — and why it matters.

~15 min read CS Students ELI5 · Deep Dive

There are two ways a machine can relate to a photograph. It can look at one and tell you what it sees. Or it can sit quietly, study thousands of them, and then produce one from nothing. The second ability is newer, stranger, and increasingly central to how AI works.

Classical computer vision is discriminative. You feed it an image, it produces a label, a bounding box, a mask. The model learns a mapping from pixels to meaning. This is powerful and useful, and it is the foundation of every facial recognition system, every medical image classifier, every self-driving car that identifies a stop sign.

Generative AI works in reverse. Instead of reading images, it writes them. It learns the structure of visual data so deeply that it can sample new examples from that structure — images that are statistically indistinguishable from photographs of the real world, yet depict things, people, and places that have never existed.

Think of an art student who spends years studying thousands of portraits. Eventually, they do not need a model to sit for them. They paint faces from memory, from imagination, from a deep understanding of what a face is.

The Generative Shift

This is not a metaphor about creativity. It is a precise description of what these models do mathematically. They learn a probability distribution over images, and generating a new image means sampling from that distribution.

Why It Matters

The Problem These Models Solve

The most pressing limitation of modern machine learning is data. Supervised models require enormous quantities of labeled examples, and collecting those examples is expensive, slow, and sometimes genuinely impossible. You cannot easily gather thousands of MRI scans of a rare tumor. You cannot wait years to collect enough footage of foggy highway collisions to train a safer car.

Generative models answer this problem directly. They produce synthetic data that is realistic enough to substitute for, or augment, real data. They allow engineers to manufacture the training sets that the world has not yet provided.

Beyond data augmentation, generative models power some of the most visible AI applications of the past few years. The photorealistic face of a person who does not exist. The photograph conjured from a sentence of text. The damaged painting restored by a model that has internalized centuries of brushwork. These are not tricks. They are the outputs of systems that have learned, in a genuine sense, how images work.

The Architectures

Three Approaches to the Same Problem

There are three major families of generative models in computer vision. Each approaches the problem of image synthesis differently, with distinct mathematical foundations, different training dynamics, and different practical trade-offs. Understanding all three is essential to working in this field.

Generative Adversarial Networks

Ian Goodfellow published the GAN paper in 2014 while at a Montreal bar after an argument with colleagues. The idea arrived nearly complete: train two networks against each other, and let the competition drive both toward excellence.

The Generator takes a vector of random noise and maps it to an image. The Discriminator receives images — some real, some generated — and tries to classify each correctly. The Generator wants to fool the Discriminator. The Discriminator wants not to be fooled. Neither can succeed permanently, so both keep improving.

The training objective is a minimax game. The Discriminator maximizes its ability to separate real from fake. The Generator maximizes the probability that the Discriminator calls its output real.

// GAN minimax objective — Goodfellow et al., 2014 min_G max_D V(D,G) = E_x~pdata[log D(x)] + E_z~pz[log(1 − D(G(z)))] // D maximizes: classify real as 1, fake as 0 // G minimizes: make D classify fake as 1 // Nash equilibrium: G produces indistinguishable images, D outputs 0.5

The results, when training works, are remarkable. StyleGAN2 generates human faces at 1024×1024 resolution that are indistinguishable from photographs in blind tests. CycleGAN translates the visual style of images without requiring paired training examples — turning photographs of horses into zebras, summer landscapes into winter ones, without a single labeled pair.

The weakness is instability. GANs are notoriously difficult to train. The balance between Generator and Discriminator is fragile. If the Discriminator learns too fast, the Generator receives uninformative gradients and stops improving. If the Generator learns too fast, the Discriminator collapses. The phenomenon known as mode collapse — where the Generator learns to produce a few convincing images rather than a diverse range — remains an unsolved problem in many settings.

Variational Autoencoders

One year before GANs, Diederik Kingma and Max Welling published a quieter paper that approached the generative problem from a completely different direction. Where GANs are adversarial, VAEs are collaborative. Where GANs are implicit, VAEs are explicit. Where GANs produce sharp images through competition, VAEs produce smooth representations through compression.

The core idea is an encoder-decoder pair with a probabilistic bottleneck. The Encoder compresses an image into a compact representation — not a fixed point, but a distribution: a mean and a variance. The Decoder takes a sample from that distribution and reconstructs the image. The whole system is trained end-to-end to reconstruct its inputs faithfully while keeping the distribution of latent codes close to a standard normal.

Imagine describing any person with just a handful of coordinates on a map: how adventurous they are, how formal, how warm. The VAE learns to build that map for images, and to navigate it.

On Latent Spaces

The training objective, called the Evidence Lower Bound, balances two competing pressures: reconstruction fidelity and regularization of the latent space. The reparameterization trick — writing the latent sample as z = μ + σ·ε where ε is drawn from a standard normal — makes the whole pipeline differentiable, allowing backpropagation through the stochastic sampling step.

// VAE objective — Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO) L(θ,φ; x) = E_q_φ(z|x)[log p_θ(x|z)] − KL(q_φ(z|x) || p(z)) // Term 1: Reconstruction — how faithfully does the decoder recover x? // Term 2: KL divergence — keep the latent distribution close to N(0,I) Key trick: z = μ + σ·ε, ε ~ N(0,I) // Makes sampling differentiable → gradient flows through z

The practical consequence of a well-structured latent space is remarkable. You can encode two images, interpolate linearly between their latent representations, and decode each point along the path. The result is a smooth morphing sequence, visually coherent at every step. You can perform arithmetic: the latent vector of a smiling face minus a neutral face, added to the latent vector of a neutral man, approximates the latent vector of a smiling man. You can detect anomalies: images that fall far outside the training distribution produce high reconstruction error.

The limitation is image quality. VAEs tend to produce blurry outputs, a consequence of the pixel-wise reconstruction loss, which averages over uncertainty rather than committing to sharp details. For downstream tasks requiring interpretable structure, VAEs remain invaluable. For raw visual quality, they have been surpassed.

Diffusion Models

The paper that changed everything arrived in 2020. Ho, Jain, and Abbeel published Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models and demonstrated that a previously theoretical framework could match GAN quality with dramatically more stable training. Within two years, diffusion models had become the dominant paradigm in generative visual AI.

The idea is elegant to the point of seeming too simple. Take an image. Add a small amount of Gaussian noise. Add more. Keep going, for hundreds or thousands of steps, until the image is completely unrecognizable — pure noise. Then train a neural network to reverse this process: given a noisy image and a timestamp indicating how much noise has been added, predict what noise to remove.

Imagine a masterpiece painting slowly buried under snowfall. Step by step, the shapes disappear, until only white remains. A diffusion model learns to excavate that painting — to recover the image from the noise, one careful pass at a time.

On Denoising

The training loss is strikingly simple: for a randomly chosen timestep t, add the corresponding amount of noise to a training image, then minimize the mean squared error between the actual noise and the network's prediction of it.

// DDPM training objective — Ho et al., 2020 L = E_t,x₀,ε [ || ε − ε_θ(√ᾱ_t · x₀ + √(1−ᾱ_t) · ε, t) ||² ] // ε_θ: U-Net predicting the noise added at step t // ᾱ_t: cumulative noise schedule // At inference: start from z ~ N(0,I), iterate T denoising steps

The architectural backbone is a U-Net with residual connections, self-attention layers, and sinusoidal time embeddings that tell the model how noisy the current input is. For text-to-image generation, cross-attention layers inject text embeddings — typically from a model like CLIP — at multiple scales, allowing the denoising process to be guided by language.

Latent Diffusion Models, the architecture underlying Stable Diffusion, operate not on pixels directly but on the compressed representations produced by a VAE. This reduces computation by an order of magnitude while preserving perceptual quality, making high-resolution generation accessible without industrial-scale hardware.

The primary cost is inference speed. Where a GAN generates an image in a single forward pass, a diffusion model requires hundreds of sequential denoising steps. Accelerated samplers like DDIM have reduced this to fifty steps or fewer without significant quality loss, but the fundamental sequential nature of the process remains a constraint.

Applications

Where This Is Used

  • Medical Imaging Synthetic MRI and CT scans for rare conditions where real training data does not exist. Models train on generated examples without exposing patient data.
  • Face Synthesis StyleGAN2 produces photorealistic portraits of people who do not exist, used for privacy-safe training datasets and digital avatars.
  • Inpainting Remove objects from photographs and fill the region seamlessly. Adobe's Generative Fill is powered by diffusion models.
  • Text-to-Image DALL·E 3, Midjourney, and Stable Diffusion generate photorealistic or artistic images from natural language descriptions.
  • Super-Resolution ESRGAN and related models upscale images by 4× to 8×, recovering detail that was never captured in the original.
  • Anomaly Detection VAEs trained on normal manufacturing images flag defects automatically — anything the decoder cannot reconstruct well is suspicious.
  • Autonomous Driving Rare and dangerous scenarios — heavy fog, black ice, collisions — are synthesized to train safer perception systems without real-world risk.
History

How We Got Here

2013

Variational Autoencoders

Kingma and Welling introduce VAEs. The first practical framework for learning structured, continuous latent representations of visual data.

2014

Generative Adversarial Networks

Goodfellow et al. publish the GAN paper. The adversarial training idea sparks a decade of research and industrial applications.

2017–19

CycleGAN, StyleGAN

Unpaired image translation becomes practical. NVIDIA's StyleGAN produces 1024×1024 faces that pass blind tests against real photographs.

2020

DDPM — Diffusion Arrives

Ho et al. demonstrate that diffusion models match GAN quality with stable, principled training. A new paradigm is established.

2021–22

DALL·E, CLIP, Stable Diffusion

Text-to-image generation becomes accessible. Latent diffusion reduces the compute cost by an order of magnitude, opening the technology to researchers and artists worldwide.

2023–26

DALL·E 3, Sora, DiT

Text-to-video reaches photorealistic quality. Diffusion Transformers replace U-Nets. Generation becomes a core capability of multimodal foundation models.

Conclusion

Generative AI did not emerge fully formed. It is the product of three distinct research traditions — probabilistic modeling, adversarial training, and score-based diffusion — each contributing something the others lacked. VAEs gave us structure. GANs gave us sharpness. Diffusion models gave us stability and control.

The practical consequence is a field where machines can now synthesize not just images but videos, 3D scenes, and multimodal content, guided by language, conditioned on context, constrained by physics. The question is no longer whether machines can generate realistic images. It is what we choose to do with that capability, and how we build the judgment to use it well.
